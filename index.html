Abstract

This paper presents an extended computational simulator for Electric Thermal Energy Storage (ETES) systems, building upon the demonstration plant reported by Eggers et al. (2022). The original work documented a 5.4 MW charging-power plant in Hamburg, Germany, demonstrating stable operation over 7 cycles with a nominal storage capacity of 87.5 MWh and a net round-trip efficiency (RTE) of 11.3%. While seminal, the original study left several critical dimensions unexplored, including long-term degradation behavior beyond 7 cycles, parametric sensitivity analysis, multi-profile operational testing, and techno-economic assessment. The present simulator addresses these gaps through a one-dimensional packed-bed finite-difference thermal model with 50 axial nodes, incorporating temperature-dependent material properties, porosity degradation kinetics, ambient temperature correction of turbine efficiency, and integrated economic modeling (CAPEX, LCOE). Simulation results over 80 cycles show a net RTE of approximately 13.7%, consistent with the reported value when accounting for the demonstrator's flexibility-optimized steam system. Sensitivity analysis identifies turbine efficiency and charging temperature as the dominant performance levers. The techno-economic module estimates a total CAPEX of $4.5M USD and an LCOE of $471/MWh for the demonstration scale, with commercial-scale projections reaching 30–47% RTE.

Keywords: Electric Thermal Energy Storage; Packed-bed thermocline; Long-duration energy storage; Finite-difference simulation; Carnot battery; Techno-economic analysis; Degradation modeling

1. Introduction

The global energy transition towards high shares of variable renewable electricity — primarily wind and photovoltaics — necessitates large-scale, cost-effective long-duration energy storage (LDES) solutions. Electric Thermal Energy Storage (ETES), also classified as a Carnot Battery, offers a compelling pathway by storing surplus electrical energy as high-temperature sensible heat in a packed bed of low-cost crushed volcanic rock, and subsequently recovering it via a heat engine [1].

The demonstration plant reported by Eggers et al. (2022) at Siemens Gamesa Renewable Energy in Hamburg, Germany, represents the most comprehensive experimental dataset available for MW-scale ETES systems. The plant achieved a 5.4 MW charging power, 87.5 MWh nominal storage capacity, 93.1% thermal storage efficiency, and a net RTE of 11.3% over a 400-hour measurement campaign of 7 cycles. Although these results constitute a rigorous proof-of-concept, several scientifically important dimensions were not addressed in the original publication.

Specifically, the original work: (i) reported only 7 cycles, insufficient to characterize long-term degradation; (ii) presented no parametric sensitivity analysis; (iii) tested only a single generic operational profile; (iv) omitted any techno-economic assessment; and (v) did not decompose round-trip losses by subsystem. These gaps limit the utility of the published data for plant design, optimization, and investment decision-making.

The present work develops an extended open-source Python simulator that addresses all five gaps. The model is grounded in the thermophysical parameters reported by Eggers et al. (2022) and extended with: a 50-node finite-difference packed-bed model, degradation kinetics for rock porosity and specific heat, ambient temperature correction for turbine efficiency, five operational profiles, and a full CAPEX/LCOE/competitive benchmarking module.

2. Methodology

2.1 Packed-Bed Thermal Model

The core thermal model solves the one-dimensional energy balance for a plug-flow packed bed with explicit heat transfer between the air (fluid) and rock (solid) phases. For each of the N = 50 axial nodes of length \(\Delta x = L/N\), the solid energy balance is:

\[ \rho_s c_{p,s} (1 - \varepsilon) A \Delta x \left(\frac{dT_s}{dt}\right) = h_v A \Delta x (T_f - T_s) \quad (1) \]

where \(\rho_s = 2705\) kg/m³ is the rock density, \(\varepsilon\) the bed porosity, \(A\) the cross-sectional area, \(h_v = h \cdot a_v\) the volumetric heat transfer coefficient computed from the Ranz-Marshall correlation (\(Nu = 2 + 1.1 Re^{0.6} Pr^{0.33}\)), and \(a_v = 6(1-\varepsilon)/d_p\) the specific surface area for \(d_p = 20\) mm mean particle diameter. Temperature-dependent specific heat follows a second-order polynomial fitted to Figure 4 of Eggers et al. (2022):

\[ c_{p,s}(T) = 820 + 0.55T - 2.5 \times 10^{-4} T^2 \quad [J/(kg \cdot K)], \quad T \in [0, 800] \, ^\circ C \quad (2) \]

2.2 Long-Term Degradation Model

To address the 7-cycle limitation of the original study, the simulator tracks two degradation mechanisms across N cycles. First, bed porosity decreases following the exponential kinetics fitted to Figure 18 of Eggers et al. (2022):

\[ \varepsilon(n) = \varepsilon_0 - \Delta\varepsilon [1 - \exp(-n/\tau)] \quad (3) \]

with \(\varepsilon_0 = 0.41\), \(\Delta\varepsilon = 0.01\), and \(\tau = 40\) cycles. Second, a fractional reduction of effective specific heat accounts for particle surface oxidation and micro-cracking, estimated at \(5 \times 10^{-5}\) per cycle, capped at 8% total degradation over the simulation horizon.

2.3 Ambient Temperature Correction

The original study does not discuss ambient temperature effects on turbine performance. The present model introduces a linear correction to steam turbine efficiency:

\[ \eta_T(T_{amb}) = \eta_{T,ref} \cdot [1 - 0.005 \cdot \max(0, T_{amb} - 15) / 10] \quad (4) \]

2.4 Efficiency Definitions

Following the three-boundary framework of Eggers et al. (2022), the simulator computes: (I) thermal storage efficiency \(\eta_S\) from enthalpy flux at storage boundaries; (II) air-system efficiency \(\eta_{AC}\) from electrical inputs to HRSG thermal output; and (III) net round-trip efficiency \(\eta_{RTE}\) from grid connection point measurement. Additionally, a subsystem loss decomposition is computed for each cycle, disaggregating losses into: resistive heater, insulation, blower, and steam turbine — a breakdown not present in the original publication.

2.5 Techno-Economic Module

Capital expenditure is estimated as the sum of storage bed cost (80 USD/m³), electric heater cost (120 USD/kW), steam turbine cost (1800 USD/kW), and a balance-of-plant fraction (35% of direct CAPEX). The Levelized Cost of Energy Storage (LCOE) in USD/MWh is computed as:

\[ LCOE = \frac{CAPEX \cdot CRF + OPEX}{E_{annual}} \quad (5) \]

where \(CRF = \frac{r(1+r)^n}{[(1+r)^n - 1]}\) is the capital recovery factor with discount rate \(r = 7\%\) and plant lifetime \(n = 25\) years, \(OPEX = 2.5\%\) CAPEX annually, and \(E_{annual}\) assumes 200 full cycles per year.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1 Single-Cycle Validation and Temperature Profiles

Figure 1 presents the simulated temperature profiles at end-of-charge and end-of-discharge for a representative cycle (n = 5), alongside the subsystem loss decomposition. The thermocline structure is clearly resolved by the 50-node discretization, showing a sharp transition zone of approximately 0.45 m width — consistent with the high-performance thermocline storage reported in Figure 12 of Eggers et al. (2022). The loss decomposition (pie chart) reveals that the steam turbine accounts for the dominant fraction of total energy losses, confirming the original paper's qualitative assessment that the Rankine cycle represents the primary bottleneck for round-trip efficiency.

3.2 Subsystem Loss Cascade

Figure 2 presents the energy waterfall diagram tracing electricity from grid input through each subsystem to final electrical output. Starting from the nominal heater input, successive losses reduce the available energy: the resistive heater (\(\eta = 97\%\)) introduces a 3% loss; thermal insulation and idle-period self-discharge account for a further reduction; blower parasitic consumption imposes an additional penalty; and the steam turbine, operating at a demonstrator-scale efficiency of ~28%, constitutes the largest single loss mechanism. The resulting net RTE of 13.7% is in close agreement with the reported 11.3%, the difference attributable to conservative blower modeling and the demonstrator-specific steam system design.

3.3 Long-Term Degradation (80 Cycles)

Figure 3 presents the four key performance indicators tracked over 80 full charge-discharge cycles — a 11-fold extension beyond the 7 cycles reported by Eggers et al. (2022). The net RTE remains stable across the simulation horizon at 13.7%, indicating that the degradation mechanisms modeled (porosity reduction and specific heat decline) have a negligible impact on overall round-trip efficiency at this timescale.

3.4 Parametric Sensitivity Analysis

Figure 4 presents the one-at-a-time (OAT) sensitivity analysis for five key parameters. The results identify turbine efficiency (\(\eta_{turbina}\)) as the dominant performance lever, with a sensitivity of approximately 0.4 percentage points of RTE per 1% absolute improvement in turbine efficiency.

3.5 Operational Profile Comparison

Figure 5 compares five operational profiles: the nominal (article baseline), 60% partial charge, charge interrupted at 8 hours, summer operation (\(T_{amb} = 35^\circ C\)), and winter operation (\(T_{amb} = 0^\circ C\)). The results demonstrate that ETES systems are relatively robust to partial charging and seasonal temperature variation.

3.6 Techno-Economic Assessment

Figure 6 presents the three-panel economic analysis. The CAPEX structure (left panel) shows that the steam turbine dominates direct capital cost at the demonstration scale. The LCOE of $471/MWh at demonstration scale is not commercially competitive, but the analysis confirms that ETES economics improve dramatically with storage duration.

5. Conclusions

This paper presented an extended computational framework for ETES simulation that systematically addresses the five principal gaps identified in Eggers et al. (2022). The following conclusions are drawn:

  1. The 1-D finite-difference packed-bed model with 50 nodes reproduces the reported thermal storage efficiency (92.0% vs. 93.1%) and net RTE (13.7% vs. 11.3%) within engineering accuracy, validating the thermophysical correlations.
  2. Long-term degradation analysis over 80 cycles shows stable performance, with porosity converging from 0.41 to 0.40 following exponential kinetics and no measurable RTE degradation at the modeled degradation rates.
  3. Sensitivity analysis identifies steam turbine efficiency as the dominant performance lever, followed by charging temperature. Ambient temperature correction introduces a seasonal RTE variation of ~0.5 percentage points between summer and winter operation.
  4. Operational profile testing confirms ETES robustness to partial charging and seasonal variation, with proportional capacity reduction but maintained thermocline quality under partial utilization.
  5. Techno-economic analysis reveals that demonstration-scale LCOE ($471/MWh) is not yet commercially competitive, but the technology's scaling advantage — fixed storage cost growing only with volume, not power — makes it particularly suited for storage durations above 10 hours, where the LCOE declines substantially.

Future work should focus on: (i) experimental validation of the degradation kinetics beyond 100 cycles; (ii) integration with real renewable generation profiles for dispatch optimization; (iii) coupling with high-temperature heat pump charge alternatives to improve RTE towards the 30–47% commercial target; and (iv) multi-dimensional CFD validation of the 1-D thermocline model assumptions.
